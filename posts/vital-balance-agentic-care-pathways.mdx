# Agentic Care Pathways for Digital Health Platforms

Digital health platforms are being asked to do more with less: streamline care, reduce clinician burnout, and still maintain strict safety standards. Agentic care pathways provide a structured way to coordinate patient workflows with AI assistance—triaging requests, escalating risk, and guiding next steps. This post outlines a practical architecture for agentic care pathways and explains how health teams can implement them safely in production.

## Context / Background

An **agentic care pathway** is a sequence of AI‑assisted steps that guide patients and clinicians through a care process, while enforcing safety, compliance, and human review when necessary.

**Example:** A patient submits symptoms via a portal. An agent assesses urgency, schedules a tele‑visit, and alerts a clinician if red‑flag symptoms appear.

## Core Content

### H3 1) Pathway Design and Risk Segmentation

The first design step is to segment pathways by risk level. Low‑risk workflows can be automated; high‑risk cases require clinician oversight.

**Example:** An agent can handle appointment scheduling for routine check‑ups, but must escalate chest‑pain cases to a nurse within minutes.

### H3 2) Clinical Knowledge Boundaries

Agents must operate within explicit clinical boundaries. This includes:
- Approved symptom guides
- Escalation rules for red flags
- Limits on medical advice content

**Example:** The agent can provide general education but cannot diagnose or prescribe.

### H3 3) Data Integrity and Consent Tracking

Care pathways depend on trustworthy data. Systems should:
- Record consent state at each step
- Log which data sources were used
- Maintain audit trails for compliance

**Example:** A patient’s wearable data is used only after consent is confirmed and stored.

### H3 4) Human‑in‑the‑Loop Safety Gates

Safety requires explicit checkpoints where a clinician can approve or revise recommendations.

**Example:** If an agent detects high‑risk symptoms, it generates a draft assessment but waits for clinician approval before contacting the patient.

### H3 5) Outcome Measurement and Continuous Improvement

A pathway is only as good as its outcomes. Track metrics like:
- Time to clinician response
- Patient satisfaction
- Follow‑up adherence

**Example:** A weekly report shows that agent‑assisted pathways reduced response times by 28% without increasing escalations.

## Practical Implementation

Below is a simplified workflow for a patient intake pathway with agentic triage and safety gates.

```yaml
workflow:
  name: patient-intake
  steps:
    - name: collect_symptoms
      action: tool.call
      tool: "portal.collect"
    - name: triage
      action: agent.assess
      input: "Classify urgency and identify red flags"
    - name: schedule
      action: tool.call
      tool: "calendar.book"
      requireApproval: false
    - name: clinician_review
      action: tool.call
      tool: "care.review"
      requireApproval: true
```

**Step‑by‑step guidance:**
1. Define a risk model with clear escalation thresholds.
2. Restrict agent output to approved clinical guidelines.
3. Add a mandatory clinician review for high‑risk cases.
4. Log every decision and data source for auditability.
5. Continuously measure outcomes and update pathways.

## FAQ

**Q1: Can agentic pathways replace clinicians?**
A: No. They support clinicians by reducing administrative load and highlighting risk.

**Q2: How do we prevent unsafe advice?**
A: Enforce policy‑based output limits and require human review for risk cases.

**Q3: What data should be logged for compliance?**
A: Inputs, outputs, consent state, and escalation decisions.

**Q4: How do we measure success?**
A: Track response time, adherence, and patient satisfaction over time.

## Key Takeaways

- Agentic care pathways improve efficiency but must be safety‑first.
- Risk segmentation and clinician gates are non‑negotiable.
- Consent and audit trails protect patients and providers.
- Outcome tracking ensures continuous improvement.
